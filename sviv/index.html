About Sviv

Elastic rhythm tool for more than just keeping time.

Fot is Norwegian for "foot" ‚Äì meaning rhythm, grounding, and the human connection to time through movement. Also the verbal expression "f√• fot" means to "get the feel" or get exited about a rhythm.

Overview

FOT is an elastic metronome and rhythm tool designed for musicians who want to explore living rhythms, asymmetric time signatures, and microscopic timing variations. Instead of a fixed BPM system, FOT works with flowing cycles where rhythm can breathe ‚Äì just like rhythms actually are in practice.

The app gives you insight into what swings because it's even ‚Äì and what swings precisely because it isn't. With adjustment possibilities down to percentage decimals, you'll find the answer to where the breaking point lies between precision and groove.

When you use SVIV, you can:

  • Create your own rhythms with any length, accent, and subdivision
  • Practice with crooked time signatures like Pols, Polska, and Springar
  • Explore cycles where each beat can have different duration
  • Run multiple instances simultaneously ‚Äì in sync, or at their own tempo
  • Use the built-in sequencer's "follow" mode to play precise bass lines over the rhythm the metronome is set to
  • Use the built-in sequencer to record and create queues of complex rhythm sequences and bass lines

Use as:

  • A standalone asymmetric metronome
  • A MIDI beat machine and sequencer, with its own tones, groove, and timing

Applications

Performers

Performers can use FOT to explore microstructures in rhythm and learn how to pull individual beats to create life in the groove.

The app makes it easy to work with asymmetric time signatures ‚Äì such as in Pols, Polska, Valdres- and Telespringar ‚Äì and other living folk dance forms.

With the built-in sequencer, you can shape precise rhythmic sequences for practice, experiment with transformations from reference rhythms, and train both polyrhythms and different ways of perceiving swing.

The sequencer function also makes it possible to program bass lines and create song sequences, so the metronome can be used in composition, practice, and live performances.

Ensemble / Collaboration / Dance

FOT can run multiple metronomes simultaneously and send signals to different MIDI outputs, each with its own rhythmic pattern or tempo. This makes it possible to program everything from bass lines and drum sounds to complex polyrhythms and interaction between different time levels.

Since the app can send MIDI into DAWs, it's easy to route each metronome to separate channels. This way, it will be easy to send the metronomes to their own headsets, wireless in-ear systems, monitors, or other audio outputs.

The app combines sound and visual indicators, so you can interact both with and without sound. This opens FOT to creative applications that have not yet been explored ‚Äì a tool for those who want to challenge the boundaries of rhythm and collaboration.

Improvisers

For those working with improvisation, FOT is a tool for creating flexible and organic time frames. You can build patterns where core pulse can vary freely, or test alternative internal proportions in real-time. You can also assign a person to control, vary, and play with the metronomes.

With FOT you can also create extreme rhythmic landscapes with many asymmetrically subdivided beats in slow and fast tempos. Combined with a sequencer with MIDI follow function for playing on keyboard, it's easy to create insane and chaotic sounds together with the VST library you have in your DAW, for example.

Composers and arrangers

FOT lets you build polymetric and asymmetric time signatures by adjusting percentage distribution, subdivisions, and the length of each beat. You can create complex rhythm sequences, explore microscopic time shifts, and shape grooves that lie outside the standardized grid in a DAW.

When you're satisfied with the result, you can export MIDI data directly to your production environment or transfer it to a notation program for transcription and arrangement. This makes FOT a compositional tool that bridges living rhythmic expression and digital production.

FOT can also be used to analyze existing music: Tap in the rhythm from a recording, and the program will calculate microtiming, CPM, and percentage distribution ‚Äì so you can reconstruct the tempo and beats of rubato music, full of fermatas, without problems. Ideal if you need to transcribe music with free timing ‚Äì or work with arrangement and scoring for a demo that "didn't take tempo too seriously".

Music educators

FOT makes teaching rhythm, timing, and groove concrete, visual, and interactive. You can show how living time signatures differ from an even quarter pulse, visualize percentage distribution in asymmetric patterns, and give students a clear picture of what makes a rhythm uneven ‚Äì and why it can still swing.

With FOT, students can see, hear, and measure differences in timing, and gain a deeper understanding of how microtiming affects groove and musical expression. The app is suitable for both individual teaching and group work, and makes abstract concepts like percentage distribution, polyrhythms, and CPM concrete and accessible.

Researchers and analysts

FOT records detailed timing data such as cycles per minute (CPM), average measure time, and percentage distribution per beat. This makes it possible to analyze and compare rhythmic practices across styles, epochs, traditions, and individual performers.

With FOT you can document and visualize differences in rhythmic execution, investigate stability and consistency in tapping over time, and extract measurable data showing how microtiming varies between different musical contexts.

The tool can thus be suitable for research in ethnomusicology, rhythm analysis, and music psychology ‚Äì or as a means to explore and map personal rhythmic characteristics of individual performers.

Features and Tools

Main Window

The main window in FOT consists of an interactive metronome wheel with visual beat cards, where you can shape your rhythm directly through intuitive controls.

  • Metronome wheel (circular display): A visual representation of your cycle, where each beat is shown as a pie piece. The size of the pie piece shows how much time the beat gets of the total. You can drag the pie pieces to adjust percentage distribution, and click on them to change subdivisions.
  • Beat cards: Visual modular "cards" for each beat, with slider for weight/percentage, subdivision counter, and MIDI parameters. You can enter values directly or drag the sliders to adjust. Each beat can get its own pitch for use with external synths.
  • CPM (Cycles Per Minute): Shows how many complete cycles (measures) are played per minute. This provides a more precise tempo measurement than traditional BPM when working with asymmetric rhythms.
  • Percentage distribution: Each beat can get a portion of total measure time, shown as percentage. This makes it possible to model uneven internal structure ‚Äì so you can create rhythms with natural "swing" or asymmetric pulses.
  • Subdivision per beat: Independent control of subdivisions (up to 16 per beat) for individual beats. This lets you create complex rhythmic structures with varying levels of detail.
  • Weight and sound: Separate volume for master beats and subdivisions, with WAV-based click sound (click.wav). You can open the sound panel (W) to adjust volume and sound character.

Sequencer

FOT has a built-in sequencer that lets you record and save rhythm sequences with complex arrangements.

  • 9 separate measure loops: Record and save up to 9 different rhythmic loops, with ability to add and shape bass lines per loop. This makes it possible to build complex rhythmic arrangements.
  • 1-shot or loop queue system: Set up a sequence of your pre-saved loops, and play them in sequence or as a continuous loop. With hotkeys (1-9) you can add and edit your queues like a loop station.

FOT has a precise tap-tempo tool for capturing living timing and exporting rhythm data.

  • MIDI export: Export a long series of tapped beats as MIDI file and use them in DAWs or notation software. This function lets you, for example, import a beat map of a song recorded freely without click, and still be able to transcribe the recordings with what will look like regular notation.
